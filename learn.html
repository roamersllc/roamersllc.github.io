Where does the protein come from in a plant based diet?

A wholefood diet containing fruits, vegetables, nuts, legumes, rice, corn and so on contains plenty of protein for the human body; vegans and vegetarians get on average 70% more protein than their minimum requirement every day (based on studies interpreted by Dr Michael Greger. So getting too little protein on a plant based diet would be extremely rare and you might argue that we are so caught up with maximizing protein in our diets because of the recent diet fads of the last decade or so which have made money from promoting mainly animal based diets (high in saturated fat and low or no carbohydrates) and we should probably be more concerned with the lack of fiber, nutrients, antioxidants etc. these can be found in abundance in tons of different plant based foods - as opposed to not getting enough protein.

What about my carbon footprint?

Studies, such as the one published in the in the journal ‘Climatic Change’ in 2014, analyzed the diets of omnivores, pescatarians, vegetarians and vegans in order to measure the greenhouse gas emissions of each. Their findings show that a meat-heavy diet produces almost 250% more CO2 per day than a vegan diet. So, if you are interested in your carbon footprint, your diet could have a bigger impact than you realize.

How safe is the meat to eat?

An estimated 70% of antibiotics sold in the US are for use in meat production. The growing threat of antibiotic resistance is largely due to the misuse and overuse of antibiotics in both people and animals - though considerably more antibiotics are used in animals raised in the meat industry (just think about why those animals might need so much antibiotics).

If we are missing B12 in a plant based diet and it is a vital vitamin, surely we should be eating some meat?

Firstly, this is a personal choice.

It can be argued that we have evolved to require a small amount of animal products in our diet if B12 is so important. To me, that makes total sense because we were hunter gatherers - it is thought the majority of our calories came from plants and a small amount from hunting. My personal opinion? In an ideal world, if we could all raise our own animals, in a spacious environment, treated them well and didn't pump them full of antibiotics or feed them on food not meant for them, and were OK with killing them and preparing and eating them, I can see that this is one way to get your B12. However, I can't do that. I live in an apartment in the city, to start with! And I know I couldn't raise an animal and then eat it. So for me, meat or diary is not my chosen way of getting B12. ​

Today's modern farming practices create huge problems, from the amount of toxic waste flushed onto the land and into the oceans, to the horrific conditions in which the animals are kept and killed. A lot of people find this too difficult to stomach, either because they are concerned about putting such a low standard of meat into their bodies, for compassionate reasons or some other reason. These people should be getting their B12 elsewhere to take care of themselves (see prior question/answer for suggestions).

What about fish, is that okay to eat?

About 20% of commercially caught fish is turned into fishmeal each year (most of that, approx. 70% is directed towards fish farming, followed by pig and chicken production). It is thought that up to 40% of commercially caught fish are 'waste' (i.e. thrown back dead or dying). Recall now that in 2010, the UN Food and Agriculture Organization estimated that 75% of all fish stocks were in distress and nearing depletion, more frighteningly, they had received research which had predicted our oceans could be fishless by 2050, if things didn't change.
